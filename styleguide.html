The Alec R. Stevens Writing Style Guide

I. Purpose and Identity

Writing under the name Alec R. Stevens should convey intellectual gravity, analytical rigor, and disciplined clarity. The voice reflects a person who is:

  • Classically educated with legal and technical training
  • Institutionally literate and procedurally precise
  • Grounded in principle, precedent, and established standards
  • Technically precise rather than performative
  • Reserved, but confident in analytical judgment
  • Persuasive through rigorous reasoning, not rhetorical volume

The goal is not to impress through ornamentation, but to earn trust through technical precision, logical structure, and methodological restraint. Writing adheres to established standards including APA format and legal citation conventions where applicable.

⸻

II. Core Voice Characteristics

1. Legally and Technically Precise

  • Professional and technically exact, never vague or imprecise.
  • Avoid slang, casual phrasing, or conversational approximations.
  • Write with the precision of legal drafting and technical scholarship.
  • Every term should be defensible; every claim should be substantiated.
Preferred

"This position carries a fiduciary responsibility not only to administer enacted policy, but to safeguard institutional integrity through adherence to established governance protocols."

Avoid

"This role is super important and really about making sure things run smoothly."

⸻

2. Technical Authority

  • State positions with definitional clarity and analytical precision.
  • Avoid unnecessary qualifiers ("I think," "I feel," "it seems").
  • Authority derives from methodology, precedent, and analytical rigor.
  • Cite sources and standards when making technical or procedural claims.
Preferred

"This approach comports with established governance standards codified in Section 3.2 and aligns with controlling precedent."

Avoid

"I believe this might possibly be a good idea."

⸻

3. Analytical Objectivity

  • Even when advocating strongly, maintain analytical composure.
  • Do not sound ideological, reactionary, or emotionally driven.
  • Let logic, precedent, established principles, and technical analysis do the work.
  • Distinguish clearly between descriptive analysis and normative judgment.
⸻

III. Tone Calibration by Context

Legal / Policy Writing (Primary Voice)

  • Exact wording matters; every term must be precise and defensible.
  • Favor definitions, enumerated sections, and parallel structure.
  • Avoid ambiguity, metaphors, or emotionally loaded terms.
  • Use legal citation conventions and reference controlling authority.
  • Structure arguments around elements, standards, and burden of proof.

Technical / Academic Writing

  • Analytical, methodologically precise, and evidence-centered.
  • Adhere to APA format and disciplinary conventions.
  • Demonstrates technical mastery without condescension.
  • Avoid rhetorical flourishes unless they clarify a technical concept.

Institutional / Administrative Writing

  • Clear authority grounded in procedural and technical literacy.
  • Language should reflect respect for hierarchy, policy, and continuity.
  • Write as a steward and technical expert, not an agitator.
  • Reference specific bylaws, codes, and regulations.

Public or Representational Writing

  • Polished, dignified, and technically accessible without sacrificing precision.
  • Avoid trend-driven language or rhetorical grandstanding.
  • Speak for institutions with technical accuracy, not over them.
⸻

IV. Sentence Construction

1. Prefer Precision Over Brevity

  • Sentences may be long if they remain logically ordered and technically precise.
  • Use commas, em dashes, and semicolons deliberately to structure complex technical reasoning.
  • Complex subordinate clauses are acceptable when they enhance analytical precision.
Example

"While the proposal addresses short-term operational needs, it does not sufficiently account for long-term governance implications as enumerated in Section 4.3(b) of the institutional bylaws."

⸻

2. Strong Verbs, Minimal Adverbs

  • Let verbs carry meaning.
  • Adverbs should be rare and purposeful.
Preferred

"The committee determined…"

Avoid

"The committee very clearly and strongly felt…"

⸻

3. Parallelism

  • Lists and clauses should mirror each other grammatically.
Example

"The policy ensures consistency, promotes accountability, and protects institutional autonomy."

⸻

V. Word Choice and Diction

Preferred Lexical Traits

  • Legal, technical, and classical academic vocabulary
  • Words that convey precision, authority, and technical understanding
  • Technical terminology used accurately and consistently
  • Legal terms of art employed with definitional precision

Examples of Preferred Vocabulary

  • Fiduciary (duty, responsibility)
  • Precedent / Stare decisis
  • Comport / Accord / Align (with standards)
  • Enumerated / Codified
  • Substantive / Procedural
  • Material (in legal sense)
  • Deliberative / Adjudicative
  • Pursuant to / In accordance with
  • Precedential / Authoritative
  • Institutional integrity
  • Governance protocols
  • Administrative competence

Avoid

  • Buzzwords and trend language
  • Corporate jargon without substance
  • Excessive emotional descriptors

Avoid

  • "Innovative" (unless clearly defined)
  • "Game-changing"
  • "Empowering"
  • "Vibes," "energy," "passion" (unless contextually justified)
⸻

VI. Structure and Organization

1. Formal Architectural Structure

  • Use hierarchical headings, enumerated sections, and formal numbering systems.
  • Documents should feel methodically architected according to established conventions.
  • Follow APA format for academic work; Bluebook or similar for legal citations.
  • Internal cross-references should use section numbers, not vague descriptors.

2. Logical Analytical Progression

Typical progression for legal/technical analysis:

  1. Context and Governing Standards
  2. Statement of Issue or Question Presented
  3. Applicable Rules, Principles, or Governing Standards
  4. Application and Analysis
  5. Implications and Counterarguments
  6. Conclusion or Recommended Action
⸻

VII. Rhetorical Style

1. Judicial Reasoning

  • Build arguments methodically from established principles to specific applications.
  • Ground claims in precedent, statutory authority, or technical standards.
  • Address counterarguments directly and analytically.
  • Use syllogistic reasoning: major premise, minor premise, conclusion.

2. Evidence-Based Argumentation

  • Every assertion should be supported by citation, data, or technical authority.
  • Distinguish between fact and interpretation.
  • Use precise quantification rather than vague generalizations.
  • Avoid hyperbole and unsubstantiated claims.

3. Technical Precision in Persuasion

  • Persuasion derives from analytical rigor, not rhetorical force.
  • Let the technical analysis carry the argument.
  • Use measured language even when conclusions are strong.
Example

"The proposed action raises material procedural concerns. First, it contravenes Section 2.4(a) of the governance bylaws. Second, it deviates from established precedent without articulated justification. Third, it creates ambiguity in the application of standards enumerated in Policy 7."

⸻

VIII. Formatting & Presentation

  • Favor clean, professional formatting consistent with APA, Bluebook, or institutional standards.
  • Use standard punctuation; avoid emojis or stylized symbols in formal writing.
  • Quotation marks for direct citations; italics for case names, book titles, and technical terms.
  • Use footnotes or endnotes for citations and technical clarifications.
  • Number sections hierarchically (I.A.1.a) for complex documents.
  • Include table of contents for documents exceeding 10 pages.
⸻

IX. What the Writing Should Signal About Alec R. Stevens

When someone reads writing authored by Alec R. Stevens, they should conclude:

  • This person commands legal and technical literacy.
  • This person understands institutions, governing principles, and procedural systems.
  • This person respects precedent but analyzes it rigorously.
  • This person speaks with definitional precision because words carry legal and technical weight.
  • This person can be trusted with substantive responsibility and complex analysis.
  • This person does not posture—he analyzes, adjudicates, and persuades through technical mastery.
⸻

X. Final Governing Principle

Write as someone who expects their words to be preserved, scrutinized, cited, and acted upon.

Nothing should sound disposable.
Nothing should sound rushed.
Nothing should sound borrowed.
Nothing should be technically imprecise.

Every document should withstand legal review, technical critique, and institutional scrutiny.
